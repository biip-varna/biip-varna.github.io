Summary of the Interview with Political Scientist Dr. Dimitar Keranov

This text summarises an interview with Bulgarian political scientist Dr. Dimitar Keranov on Bulgarian–Moldovan relations, regional security, and international dynamics in Southeast Europe after Russia’s large-scale military invasion of Ukraine.

Key points:

  • Moldova’s Europeanisation: Speaking from Chisinau, Dr. Keranov notes continued economic and social progress toward the EU despite corruption and Russian influence. In his view, Moldova is politically ready and could realistically join the EU in about five years, provided there are no dramatic domestic political shifts.
  • Challenges and Russian disinformation: He describes societal division and underlines Russia’s role as a major driver of disinformation—particularly in Russian-speaking areas such as Gagauzia and Taraclia— where false narratives are used to discredit President Maia Sandu and deepen artificial cleavages. On Transnistria, he argues the region should not block Moldova’s EU future and may eventually reintegrate under a political settlement.
  • Bulgaria’s role: Bulgarian–Moldovan relations have greater strategic potential than is often acknowledged, as Moldova is an increasingly important element of European security. Bulgaria—an EU and NATO member—can assist against hybrid threats, share lessons from energy diversification, and provide administrative expertise. He highlights the success of the University of Ruse branch in Taraclia and the cultural centre in Comrat, while noting that Bulgaria’s domestic political instability and governance challenges constrain a coherent long-term strategy.
  • Romanian–Bulgarian cooperation: Dr. Keranov argues that expanding Romania–Bulgaria cooperation—especially in the Black Sea region—is essential in responding to Russian aggression and information warfare. Both countries have a strong interest in Moldova’s EU accession and should consider joint initiatives in support of Moldova, including in resilience against hybrid attacks.
  • Energy and infrastructure: Bulgaria is described as a “European gateway” to gas corridors, enabling access to LNG via Greece and strengthening Moldova’s energy security. He welcomes the December 2025 memorandum signed by Bulgaria, Greece, and Romania on infrastructure corridors between the Black and Aegean Seas, framing it as a practical mechanism to improve regional coordination, logistics, and security in a context shaped by the war in Ukraine.
  • Foreign policy vision: Dr. Keranov advocates a modern Bulgarian foreign policy based on:
    • Strong support for Ukraine and Moldova.
    • Real connectivity (transport, energy, digital) to reduce vulnerabilities within NATO and to eliminate dependence on Soviet-era weapons systems.
    • Defence against hybrid threats, drawing lessons from Moldova’s targeted efforts to neutralise Russian interference.
    He concludes that Bulgaria and Romania should be among the most active countries in expanding cooperation across all areas.
  • Dimitar Keranov’s initiatives: He refers to founding the Bulgarian Institute for International Policy (a think tank in Varna) and serving as one of the presidents of the Institute of Political Science. He also maintains a Romanian-language Facebook page—“International Politics Made Simple”—aimed at presenting Bulgarian perspectives to Moldovan audiences and communicating the practical benefits of EU membership.
